Past Practices

A past practice is a longstanding, consistent practice that is accepted and known by both union and management. Past practices may establish conditions of employment that will be considered part of the CBA, and grievances may be filed if management violates them. The following factors reflect some FLRA case law on how to recognize a past practice that establishes a condition of employment: 

  • Is it openly and consistently exercised?
  • Does it cover a significant period of time?
  • Is it acknowledged and accepted by both parties, or followed by one and not challenged by the other?

If a past practice exists that is shown to be contrary to law or regulation, the agency may terminate the practice even if the union does not agree on the substance of that decision.  However, the termination of the practice may give rise to a duty to bargain over the impact on employees.

Examples of potentially binding past practices include:  ordering out and delivery of food for lunch or retirement receptions on duty time; displaying of food and refreshments at breaks; and office picnics on duty time, etc.

Keep in mind, a past practice is something not reduced to writing in a CBA.
